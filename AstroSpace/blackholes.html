Black Holes

Black holes are regions in space with gravitational pull so strong that nothing, not even light, can escape from them.

  • Black holes form when massive stars collapse under their own gravity.
  • There are different types of black holes: stellar, supermassive, and intermediate.
  • The event horizon is the boundary around a black hole beyond which nothing can escape.
  • Supermassive black holes are believed to exist at the center of most galaxies.
  • Black holes can be detected by observing the effects of their gravitational pull on nearby objects.
  • The size of a black hole is determined by its event horizon, known as the Schwarzschild radius.
  • Time slows down near a black hole due to the immense gravitational field.
  • Black holes are not empty; they contain an enormous amount of mass.
  • Scientists use various methods, including gravitational waves, to study black holes.
  • Theories about black holes were first proposed by Albert Einstein’s theory of general relativity.
